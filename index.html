Constraints

I want to implement this vision. But when we start talking about implementation, which we did in the sprint days that followed SotM, we can no longer ignore the constraints. We had to think about how to get there from where we are now, taking into account the time and technical resources we have.

Connecting communities

OSM is an open data project -- we focus on the data first and foremost, and leave the mapping (and all the other uses) to third parties, with the hope that those third party services, or their users, contribute back to OSM. We try to mandate that throught the license terms -- but in fact virtually nothing has ever been contributed back through force of license. People give back because it benefits them, and because it's easy to and fun to do. And the latter is the goal with the design changes we're making -- make it easy and fun for users of other communities, who may not even have known that they were using OSM data, to become contributors.

Change

The theme of this State of the Map is Change, and since what we're talking about here is change to the website, I wanted to do a little sidebar about change itself. It's interesting to read about how people think about and respond to change, and apply that psychology to software development. I can't go into detail, but if this sounds interesting to you too, read about: "loss aversion" -- which is a tendency to strongly prefer avoiding losses to acquiring gains. And "status quo bias", a preference for the current state of affairs.

Status Quo Bias

We get feedback. The majority of it is positive, helpful, constructive, and thoughtful, and has improved what we've done immensely. Thank you to everyone who has contributed in this way -- many of you are here this weekend. It feels great to get this kind of feedback.

But -- not everyone will like every change, and some people seemingly dislike like most every change, and are not afraid to express their dislike, unconstructively. I think this is largely a phenomenon of mailing lists. I've never gotten unconstructive criticism from someone I've had beers with or met in person -- these are by and large community members who don't attend conferences. I don't have a solution for this. But, this does have an effect on one's self esteem, so I just want to say -- be moderate, and, moderate and continue to call out bad behavior on mailing lists. Thank you!
